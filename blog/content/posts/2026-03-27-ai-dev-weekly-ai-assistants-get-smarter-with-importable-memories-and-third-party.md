This week in AI-assisted software development, we saw significant updates to AI assistants, including the ability to import memories and integrate with third-party chatbots. [According to The Verge AI](https://www.theverge.com/ai-artificial-intelligence/902085/google-gemini-import-memory-chat-history), Google's Gemini is rolling out new features to import memories and chat histories, making it easier for users to switch between AI models. Meanwhile, [Apple is reportedly allowing third-party chatbots to plug into Siri](https://www.theverge.com/tech/902048/apple-siri-ai-chatbot-update-ios-27), opening up new possibilities for AI-assisted development.


## Importable Memories for AI Assistants

Google's Gemini is making it easier to import memories and chat histories from other AI models. This feature, [available on desktop](https://www.theverge.com/ai-artificial-intelligence/902085/google-gemini-import-memory-chat-history), allows users to quickly copy over their current AI's knowledge and preferences. To use the 'Import Memory' feature, users can simply navigate to the Gemini settings and select the 'Import Memory' option. [According to the Google AI Blog](https://blog.google/innovation-and-ai/models-and-research/gemini-models/gemini-3-1-flash-live/), Gemini 3.1 Flash Live also introduces new audio AI capabilities, making it more natural and reliable.


## Third-Party Integrations for AI Assistants

Apple's iOS 27 update will allow users to choose the AI chatbot they want to link with Siri. [According to Bloomberg's Mark Gurman](https://www.theverge.com/tech/902048/apple-siri-ai-chatbot-update-ios-27), third-party chatbots like Google's Gemini or Anthropic's Claude will be able to fetch replies for Siri. This opens up new possibilities for AI-assisted development, enabling developers to create more integrated and seamless user experiences. For example, developers can use the [Siri API](https://developer.apple.com/siri/) to integrate their chatbots with Siri and create custom voice commands.


## AI Agents on a Budget

In a fascinating experiment, [a developer put an AI agent on a $7/month VPS with IRC as its transport layer](https://georgelarson.me/writing/2026-03-23-nullclaw-doorman/). This project demonstrates the feasibility of running AI agents on low-cost infrastructure, making it more accessible to developers and hobbyists. To try this out, developers can use a library like [IRC.js](https://github.com/IRCjs/irc.js) to create an IRC client and integrate it with their AI agent.


## Looking Ahead

As AI assistants continue to evolve, we can expect to see more innovative features and integrations. With the ability to import memories and integrate with third-party chatbots, AI assistants are becoming more powerful and flexible tools for developers. As we look to the future, it's exciting to think about the possibilities that these advancements will enable, from more seamless user experiences to more efficient development workflows. [According to James Manyika's conversation with LL COOL J](https://blog.google/innovation-and-ai/technology/ai/ll-cool-j-dialogues/), AI and creativity will continue to intersect and drive innovation in the years to come.
